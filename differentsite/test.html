Meteor Showers

Trails of
Cosmic Dust

When Earth passes through the debris trails left by comets, tiny grains of rock and ice enter our atmosphere at 70 km/s, burning up in brilliant streaks of light we call shooting stars.

Perseids
Peaks mid-August · Up to 100 meteors/hr · Parent: Comet Swift-Tuttle
Leonids
Peaks mid-November · Up to 15 meteors/hr · Parent: Comet Tempel-Tuttle
Geminids
Peaks mid-December · Up to 120 meteors/hr · Parent: Asteroid 3200 Phaethon
Constellations

Ancient Maps
of the Sky

For millennia, civilizations connected the dots between distant suns to chart the seasons, tell stories, and navigate the seas. 88 official constellations still guide us today.

Orion
The Hunter
One of the most recognizable constellations, home to the brilliant supergiants Betelgeuse and Rigel.
Ursa Major
The Great Bear
Contains the famous Big Dipper asterism, used for thousands of years to locate Polaris, the North Star.
Scorpius
The Scorpion
The red supergiant Antares marks the scorpion's heart, rivaling Mars in its fiery crimson brilliance.
Cassiopeia
The Queen
The unmistakable W-shape of Cassiopeia is visible year-round in the northern hemisphere, tracing the queen's throne.
Cygnus
The Swan
Also called the Northern Cross. Deneb, its brightest star, is one of the most luminous stars visible to the naked eye.
Leo
The Lion
Regulus marks the lion's heart. In spring, Leo rides high in the sky and is the radiant point of the Leonid meteor shower.
Stars

The Furnaces
of the Universe

Stars are massive spheres of plasma, held together by gravity and powered by nuclear fusion. Their color reveals their temperature — blue-white the hottest, deep red the coolest.

Class O — Blue Giants
Extremely rare, luminous, and short-lived. The universe's most massive and brilliant stars.
> 30,000 K
Class A — White Stars
Sirius and Vega belong here. Bright and prominent, visible across vast distances.
7,500–10,000 K
Class G — Yellow Dwarfs
Our own Sun falls here. Stable, long-lived stars perfectly suited for sustaining life.
5,200–6,000 K
Class M — Red Giants
Betelgeuse and Antares — cool, enormous, nearing the end of their stellar lives.
< 3,700 K
